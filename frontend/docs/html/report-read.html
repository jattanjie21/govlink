Where The Gambia's healthcare access actually breaks down.

Mapping facility distance against population density across 120 wards reveals what averages have been hiding for a decade — and the regions where the next health-sector budget could move the most.

AT Aminata TourayHealth Policy Desk · GovLink · Published 28 Apr 2025 · Updated 02 May 2025
In this report

  2. Where the gap actually is
  3. Why CRR-North is different

Built on3 datasets: Health Facility Census 2024, Population & Housing Census 2023, and the GBoS Ward Geometry pack.

The Ministry of Health's headline figure for facility access has barely moved in five years. In 2019, the average Gambian lived 6.4 km from the nearest healthcare facility. In 2024, that average is 6.1 km — a 4.7% improvement that, if you stop reading there, looks like quiet progress. But averages, as anyone who has waited three hours at a rural health post will tell you, can be the most misleading number in a national dataset. The figure obscures the fact that more than a quarter of the country's wards have actually moved further from care over the same period.

The 2024 Health Facility Census is the first national register that pairs every one of The Gambia's 728 healthcare facilities with a verified geocoded location. Cross-referenced against the 2023 Population & Housing Census ward boundaries, it lets us — for the first time — compute genuine access metrics at the ward level rather than the regional average. The results are sharper than the headline suggests, and in some places they are alarming.

Where the gap actually is

The clearest way to see the picture is the scatter at the top of this report. Plotting facility distance against population density places every ward on a grid where the policy goal — short distance, served population — sits in the lower-right. The upper-left corner is the access blackspot: low density, long distance, no clinic on the horizon. There are 23 wards in that quadrant. Twenty-one of them are in Upper River Region and the northern part of Central River Region.

The headline 6.1 km figure is not wrong. It is just an average across two countries — the urbanised South-West, and the eastern wards where access has stagnated since the late 2000s. Dr. Lamin Sanyang · Director of Public Health, MoH · in conversation with GovLink, 11 April 2025

The 23 access blackspots together hold roughly 148,000 people — about 5.4% of the national population, but consuming 14% of the country's reported maternal-health emergency caseload. They are also the wards where the distance to facility figure has actually grown since 2019, as small posts have closed without replacement.

Why CRR-North is different

Central River Region North looks at first like a typical eastern access problem — long distances, low density, sparse facilities. But the trajectory is different. Where Upper River has stagnated, CRR-North's average distance has grown 22% in five years, the worst regression on the map. The reason is straightforward when you look at the facility-type data alongside it.

Of CRR-North's 62 active facilities in 2019, 14 — almost a quarter — closed or were downgraded by 2024. Most were single-staff health posts that served a handful of nearby villages. When they shut, the catchment didn't disappear; it just got handed to the next-nearest facility, often 9 km further away. The effect is statistical: a region that did not become poorer or sparser nonetheless saw its access metric collapse, simply because a particular tier of small infrastructure dissolved without a successor.

This is not a story about underfunding alone. The Ministry's own 2023 sector review flagged the supply-side problem: the small post is a hard institution to staff, retain, and supply. What the new census makes possible is the demand-side picture — which specific 23 wards are now living with a service void, and what tier of facility the void implies.

What the data implies for budget 2026

If the Ministry's stated goal of no Gambian more than 8 km from a facility is to mean anything, the 2026 budget will need to address the upper-left of the scatter directly. Three things follow from the data:

  1. Targeted small-post reinstatement. Reopening 9 of the closed CRR-North posts — chosen by ward-level catchment, not by district equity — would bring 78,000 people back inside the 8 km policy line. The unit cost is well-documented; the ward selection has not been data-driven before.
  2. A second tier above the post. Twelve of the 23 blackspot wards are too sparse for a permanent post but sit within an hour's drive of an existing district hospital. A monthly mobile-clinic schedule covering those twelve, anchored on the existing district facilities, addresses the access gap without standing up new infrastructure.
  3. A pre-emptive map for closures. The Ministry approves facility downgrades each year on operational grounds. The new ward-level access data lets the same approval process show, in advance, which closures would push their catchment past the 8 km threshold — and require a compensating intervention before the closure goes through.
"The frustrating thing has always been knowing the average. We knew the average. We could not act on the average. With the ward-level census in hand, the conversation about where the next two billion dalasis goes is, for the first time, a conversation about specific places." — Adama Sallah, Public Finance Adviser, Ministry of Finance · 14 Apr 2025

Methodology

Distances are computed as the Haversine straight-line distance from each ward's population-weighted centroid to the nearest active facility. Active is defined as registered in the 2024 Health Facility Census with a non-null last_inspected date in the previous 24 months. All calculations were run against the 1.4.2 release of the GovLink Ward Geometry pack and reproduced against the MoH's 2023 sector review numbers, which agree to within 0.4 km at the regional level.

Underlying data and the analysis notebook are open. The full ward-level access table is published as a supplementary release (access-by-ward-2024.csv) and the Python notebook is available on the GovLink GitHub. Reproduction takes about six minutes on a laptop.

This analysis was reviewed by the MoH Department of Public Health on 24 April 2025 and the GBoS Demography Unit on 25 April 2025. Any errors of interpretation are the author's.
